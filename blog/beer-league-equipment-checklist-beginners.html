Beer League Equipment Checklist for Beginners

July 6, 2026 • 7 min read • By Utah Glizzies HC

You've decided to play beer league hockey. Congratulations, you now need to become a temporary equipment expert. The good news: you don't need to spend like an NHL scratch to gear up. You need the right pieces, in the right order of priority, bought the right way. Here's exactly what to get, what to skip, and where to save your money for beer instead.

The Full Checklist

Here's everything on the ice with you, roughly in order of how much it matters to get right:

  • Skates — the single most important piece of gear. Fit matters more than brand or price.
  • Helmet with cage or shield — most beer leagues require full facial protection, no exceptions.
  • Stick — flex, curve, and length matter more than the price tag.
  • Shoulder pads — protect collarbones and ribs from the boards and errant elbows.
  • Elbow pads — cheap to buy used, easy to overlook, genuinely important.
  • Gloves — protect your hands from slashes and pucks; fit and flexibility beat flash.
  • Hockey pants — protect hips and thighs; sizing runs differently than street pants.
  • Shin guards — should overlap your skates with no gap at the ankle.
  • Jock or jill — non-negotiable, buy new, no debate here.
  • Neck guard and mouthguard — cheap, required by most leagues, zero reason to skip.
  • Hockey socks and a base layer — moisture-wicking fabric keeps you from freezing between shifts.
  • Hockey bag — anything that holds it all and doesn't fall apart works fine.

Where to Spend, Where to Save

You don't need pro-level gear to play beer league — you need smart choices on the pieces that actually affect safety and performance. Skates should be bought new and properly fitted; a bad-fitting pair will ruin your first season regardless of price. Your helmet and mouthguard should also be new, for obvious reasons.

Everything else has room to flex. Shoulder pads, elbow pads, and hockey pants wear out slowly and are commonly sold gently used at gear swaps, secondhand shops, and online marketplaces. Your hockey bag can be the cheapest large duffel that holds everything — nobody's judging your bag.

The Stick Rule Every Beginner Should Know

Here's the counterintuitive part: skip the $300 premium stick. Beginners break, outgrow, or simply misjudge sticks constantly while their game is developing, and a single expensive twig snapping mid-season stings a lot more than a mid-range one doing the same. The better move is buying two or three solid mid-range sticks across the season instead of gambling on one top-tier model. We go deeper on exact stick picks, flex, and curve advice in our guide to the best hockey sticks for beer league in 2026.

The goal isn't to look like a pro. It's to show up protected, comfortable, and not broke by October.

A Realistic Budget Approach

  1. Start with loaner or rental gear. Many adult learn-to-skate and learn-to-play programs provide loaner equipment for your first sessions — use it before buying anything.
  2. Buy the non-negotiables new. Skates, helmet, mouthguard. Get properly fitted at a shop if you can.
  3. Buy the rest used or budget-new. Pants, shoulder pads, elbow pads, gloves, shin guards, bag.
  4. Add a mid-range stick or two. Save the premium stick purchase for once you actually know your preferred flex and curve.

If you're still working through the learn-to-play stage, there's no rush to buy anything beyond skates and a helmet — most programs cover the rest while you're learning. We rounded up every rink running adult beginner programs in our guide to hockey lessons for adults in Salt Lake City.

One Last Tip: Smell Management Starts Day One

Whatever you buy, air it out after every session instead of leaving it zipped in the bag. Gear smell is a beer league legend for a reason, and the habit of drying your equipment properly from week one will save your car, your closet, and your teammates' noses for years to come.
